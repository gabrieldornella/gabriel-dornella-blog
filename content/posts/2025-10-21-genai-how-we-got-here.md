It’s truly fascinating to witness the rapid evolution of Artificial Intelligence over the past few years. We are, in all likelihood, living through a transformation as profound as the popularization of the Internet in the 1990s or the smartphone revolution of the 2010s.

Although AI has existed for decades, the most recent wave of progress came with Generative AI, which gained real traction less than ten years ago. In just a short period, we’ve gone from models that merely understood text to models capable of reasoning, creating multimodal content, and interacting autonomously with other systems.

## How It All Began: The Publication of “Attention Is All You Need”

In 2017, researchers at Google published the paper [“Attention Is All You Need”](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762), introducing a new architecture for processing natural language called the Transformer. Unlike previous architectures, which analyzed text word by word and sequentially, the Transformer can analyze the entire context at once. It calculates the weight of the relationship (attention) between each word and every other word, allowing it to capture subtle connections of meaning and context.

In practice, this meant that AI began to understand not only the individual meaning of words but also how each relates to all the others in a text. It could grasp nuances, references, and connections even when ideas were far apart—such as understanding a joke told at the beginning of a story or following a long narrative without “losing the thread.”

## The First Transformer-Based Models

In 2018, the first models using the Transformer architecture appeared. Google launched BERT, which showed enormous potential in language comprehension tasks like search and text classification. OpenAI released GPT-1, a generative model trained to predict the next word and thus produce fluent text.

In 2019, GPT-2 drew global attention for generating surprisingly coherent text, so much so that OpenAI delayed its full release due to concerns about potential misuse.

These advances paved the way for increasingly large and versatile models like GPT-3 (2020), capable of producing coherent text across a wide range of styles and contexts.

## Making Language Models More Useful with RLHF

Despite their power, these models still made frequent mistakes, such as hallucinations and difficulty following instructions. Then, in 2022, OpenAI published [“Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback”](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.02155), introducing InstructGPT, a version of GPT-3 fine-tuned with RLHF (Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback).

In simple terms, RLHF involves three stages:

- **Supervised fine-tuning:** the model is refined with examples of good responses written by humans.
- **Reward model training:** a model learns to predict how humans would rate different responses generated by a language model.
- **Reinforcement learning:** the model is optimized to maximize the score given by the reward model.

The result was a more aligned, useful, and safe language model—better at following instructions and less prone to incorrect or nonsensical answers.

## The Launch of ChatGPT

In November 2022, OpenAI released ChatGPT, allowing anyone to interact with advanced language models easily through a web interface or app. Initially based on GPT-3.5, ChatGPT combined the Transformer architecture, RLHF fine-tuning, and a simple, user-friendly interface.

ChatGPT amazed the world, reaching [100 million users](https://www.reuters.com/technology/chatgpt-sets-record-fastest-growing-user-base-analyst-note-2023-02-01/) just two months after launch—becoming the fastest-growing app in history. Its success ignited an industry-wide race, leading companies like Google and Anthropic to release their own solutions, such as Bard (later evolved into Gemini) and Claude.

## Advanced Reasoning (Chain of Thought) and World Interaction (ReAct)

As prompt engineering became popular, new techniques emerged that significantly enhanced the reasoning capabilities of these models.

- **Chain of Thought (CoT):** introduced in a [2022 paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.11903), this technique instructs the model to explain its reasoning step by step before giving the final answer, improving accuracy in logic, math, and analytical problems.
- **ReAct (Reason + Act):** introduced in a [2022 paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03629), it combines reasoning and action, allowing the model to decide what to do and then interact with its environment—such as querying an API, searching the web, or processing data.

These techniques paved the way for function calling—the ability for a model to call external functions to fetch data, process information, or trigger systems. It was like handing the AI the keys to access the outside world, enabling real-time search, code execution, and file analysis. This shift would be crucial for the emergence of autonomous agents.

## The Rise of Autonomous Agents

By combining advanced reasoning with function calling, the concept of autonomous AI agents emerged—systems capable of conversing, planning, executing actions, and continuously adapting based on interactions. More than chatbots, these agents have clear goals, memory, access to tools, and the ability to make decisions to complete tasks.

Today, autonomous agents are one of the main focus areas for AI investment, taking several forms:

- **Personal agents**, such as Gemini, Claude, and ChatGPT, acting as multimodal general assistants.
- **Enterprise agents**, integrated into service, sales, and operations systems, like Salesforce’s Agentforce, improving customer experience.
- **Specialized agents**, focused on development and automation, such as Cursor, Devin AI, Claude Code, and Gemini Code Assist.

With a future of multiple agents interacting with each other, new standards have emerged—such as MCP (Model Context Protocol), which facilitates integrating agents with other systems, and A2A (Agent-to-Agent), which enables cooperation and information sharing among agents.

## What Lies Ahead

The rise of autonomous agents marks just the beginning of a new phase in Artificial Intelligence. Just as the Transformer redefined what was possible in 2017, the next wave will likely come from more proactive agents—connected to the physical world, embedded in personal devices, and operating continuously in multimodal ways.

We’re already seeing increasingly powerful, fast, and accessible Generative AI models. New advances in multimodality—such as models that can create realistic videos and interact with audio, images, and text in real time—combined with huge context windows and deep personalization, will enable AI to understand and follow our world with remarkable precision.

Some say there’s a limit to how far these models can go. Maybe one day that will be true. But even if progress stopped today, we’d already have enough technology to reinvent processes, products, and the way we work.

The best part? We’re only in year three of Generative AI. Imagine what’s still to come. For comparison, in year three of the Internet, Google didn’t exist yet, and in year three of smartphones, Uber hadn’t been created. The future is going to be truly fascinating.
