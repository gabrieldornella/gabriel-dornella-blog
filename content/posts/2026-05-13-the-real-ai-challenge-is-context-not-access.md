Artificial Intelligence is evolving faster than any technology I have seen in my career.

We are still early in this cycle, but the pace already feels different from previous waves of technology. The internet changed the world, but it took years before the major platforms, business models, and behaviors became obvious. AI is moving much faster.

In just a few years, we went from language models that could write useful text to systems that can reason through complex problems, generate images and videos, interact with tools, analyze documents, write code, and support decisions across different domains.

It is easy to look at that progress and assume the biggest challenge for companies is getting access to the right AI tool.

I do not think that is true anymore.

Access to technology is becoming easier every month. The models are getting better. The interfaces are getting simpler. More vendors are embedding AI into their products. More employees are experimenting with AI in their daily work.

The real challenge now is not access.

The real challenge is context.

And more specifically: how to integrate AI into the actual flow of work.

## Intelligence without context is not enough

There is a simple example I like.

Imagine I ask an AI assistant:

“I am 100 meters away from the car wash. Should I drive or walk there?”

A very reasonable AI system might answer:

“You should walk. It is faster, healthier, better for the environment, and avoids turning on the car for such a short distance.”

![AI needs context to be useful](/gabriel-dornella-blog/images/posts/context/ai_context_car_wash_prompt_1.png)

The answer sounds rational. It is structured. It is helpful. It is not a hallucination.

But it is wrong.

Because if I am going to the car wash, I am probably not going there to visit the building. I am going there because I want to wash the car.

A human understands that immediately because a human brings context to the question. The AI answered the literal question. The human understands the situation.

That difference matters.

It is not enough for AI to be smart. It needs to understand the work, the goal, the environment, the constraints, and the meaning behind the request.

Without that, it can produce answers that are polished, logical, and still not useful. If I had given the right context, the answer would be right.

![AI needs context to be useful](/gabriel-dornella-blog/images/posts/context/ai_context_car_wash_prompt_2.png)

## This becomes much harder inside companies

The car wash example is simple.

Now imagine the questions people ask inside companies:

“Where should I focus my attention in this portfolio?”

“What is the risk of this project?”

“What should I do next to close this customer?”

“How should we respond to this support issue?”

“What is blocking this initiative?”

These are not just knowledge questions. If you ask a generic AI model for best practices in project management, sales, or customer support, you will probably get a good answer.

But that is not the real job.

The real job is applying that knowledge to a specific situation.

What does “risk” mean in this company? Is it budget risk? Timeline risk? Legal risk? Customer risk? Reputation risk?

What does “focus” mean for this team? Should attention go to overdue tasks, strategic customers, silent stakeholders, blocked approvals, or work that looks fine on paper but is quietly losing momentum?

What does “next step” mean in a sales process? It depends on previous conversations, objections, internal politics, timing, decision criteria, relationship history, and dozens of small signals that are usually spread across emails, calls, notes, documents, CRM fields, and people’s memories.

A model can only reason well with the context it has.

And in most companies, the context is fragmented.

## We are moving from prompt engineering to context engineering

For the last few years, many of us learned how to prompt better.

We learned to tell the model who it should be, what task it should perform, what format we expect, what constraints it should follow, and what examples it should use.

![AI needs context to be useful](/gabriel-dornella-blog/images/posts/context/ai_context_prompt_engineering.png)

That still matters.

But for business problems, prompt engineering is not enough.

If I want AI to help me manage a portfolio of projects, I cannot only write a better prompt. I need to give the model the actual portfolio, the projects, the owners, the status, the updates, the dependencies, the comments, the deadlines, the documents, and the meaning of each field.

If I want AI to help with a customer opportunity, I need more than a sales framework. I need the account history, the meeting notes, the objections, the stakeholders, the buying process, the latest emails, the open questions, and the commercial context.

If I want AI to help resolve a service issue, I need the ticket history, customer profile, service level, prior incidents, product context, and operational constraints.

This is why I believe the next stage of AI adoption is less about prompt engineering and more about context engineering.

The question is no longer only:

“How do I ask the AI better questions?”

The better question is:

“How do we make the right context available at the right moment in the workflow?”

![AI needs context to be useful](/gabriel-dornella-blog/images/posts/context/ai_context_engineering.png)

## The tool alone is not the transformation

This is where many AI initiatives lose momentum.

A company buys a tool. People get excited. There are demos. There are pilots. A few individuals become much more productive.

But the way work happens does not really change.

Approvals are still manual. Information is still scattered. Decisions are still prepared from scratch. Customer handoffs still depend on people copying and pasting updates. Leaders still ask for status meetings because they do not trust the data. Teams still use AI as a side activity instead of as part of the work itself.

That is more tool adoption than transformation. And there is nothing wrong with tool adoption, but we should not confuse it with business impact.

The companies that create real value with AI will not be the ones that simply give everyone access to more tools. They will be the ones that redesign workflows around new capabilities.

They will ask:

- Where does intelligence enter the process?
- What repetitive work should disappear?
- What decisions should AI prepare, summarize, or challenge?
- Where does human judgment remain essential?
- What context does the system need to be useful?
- How do we measure whether the work improved?

That is a very different conversation from “which AI tool should we buy?”

## AI needs to live where the work happens

For AI to become useful at scale, it cannot sit outside the workflow as an isolated assistant that people visit when they remember.

It needs to live where the work happens.

Inside the project plan.

Inside the customer record.

Inside the support ticket.

Inside the knowledge base.

Inside the approval flow.

Inside the operating rhythm of the team.

That does not mean AI should make every decision. In many cases, the most valuable role for AI is not to replace the human. It is to prepare the human.

Summarize the messy context.

Surface the risks.

Identify the pattern.

Draft the response.

Recommend the next action.

Point out what changed.

Challenge assumptions.

Make the invisible visible.

Then the human applies judgment.

This is the practical version of AI adoption I believe more companies need to focus on. Not AI as a magic layer. Not AI as a feature checklist. AI as part of the operating system of work.

## A simple way to think about it

When I look at a workflow, I like to ask five questions:

1. **What is the outcome we are trying to improve?**  
   Faster response time, better forecast accuracy, higher conversion, fewer escalations, better project delivery, lower manual effort.

2. **Where is the friction today?**  
   Searching for information, writing repetitive updates, preparing meetings, interpreting status, coordinating handoffs, making decisions with incomplete context.

3. **What context would an intelligent assistant need?**  
   Data, documents, history, definitions, business rules, stakeholder input, prior decisions, and current status.

4. **What should AI do, and what should humans still own?**  
   AI can summarize, classify, recommend, draft, compare, and monitor. Humans should own judgment, accountability, ethics, relationships, and final decisions where the stakes require it.

5. **How will we know if the workflow got better?**  
   If we cannot measure the change, we probably have an interesting demo, not an operating improvement.

This is not as glamorous as saying “we implemented AI.”

But it is much more useful.

## The real shift

The most important AI question for leaders is changing.

It used to be:

“What can this tool do?”

Now it is becoming:

“How should work change because this capability exists?”

That is the shift.

AI is not only a new category of software. It is a new capability that can be embedded into how companies operate, decide, serve customers, and coordinate teams.

But that only happens when we give it context.

The future of AI in companies will not be defined only by who has access to the best model. It will be defined by who can connect intelligence to the flow of work.

That is where the real value is.

And that is where the hard work begins.
