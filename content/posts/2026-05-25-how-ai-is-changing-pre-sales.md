The role of solution engineers and pre-sales professionals is going through one of the biggest transformations I have seen in my career.

![AI-powered pre-sales demo architecture](/gabriel-dornella-blog/images/future-presales-architecture.png)

When I started in this field in 2004, still as an intern, a large part of my work was preparing or fixing the environments that the pre-sales team used to build demos.

Everything was still on-premise. I spent a lot of time configuring virtual machines so we could have the database, the application server, the web server, and all the other components working properly. After that, I had to prepare data and create the configurations the pre-sales team needed to build their demonstrations.

Later, when I became a full-time employee, I still had to maintain my own virtual machines, but I started spending my time building the demos themselves. More than half of my time was dedicated to maintaining environments, creating demonstrations, or adapting existing ones.

Over time, cloud solutions changed part of that work. The time spent managing environments, updating servers, and copying virtual machines mostly disappeared because the cloud handled much of that for us.

But one problem remained: building a new demo still took a lot of time.

Depending on the situation, pre-sales professionals often had to adapt an existing demo by changing a logo, a few names, or some small details because there simply was not enough time to create something fully customized for a new customer or a new business scenario.

After many years managing pre-sales teams, I saw this pattern very clearly. I even had reports measuring it: 30% or 40% of the team's time was spent building solutions.

That included thinking through the best architecture, configuring software, creating data sets, personalizing screens, building reports, and connecting all of it into a story that made sense for the customer presentation.

Now, with the new generation of AI tools, I believe our role is entering a major transformation.

Recently, I had a situation where I did not have anyone from the team available to prepare a customer presentation, so I decided to do it myself. The discovery call had been handled by an SDR, which meant my first interaction with the customer would already be the solution presentation.

The obvious approach would have been to deliver a generic overview of the platform and its capabilities, but I decided to try something different.

I connected Codex to the MCP of our notetaker, so it could access the transcript of the call between the SDR and the customer. Then I added more context about the company, its competitors, what I wanted to show, and the type of story I wanted to tell.

Codex was also connected to the MCP of our platform. In practice, Codex became the pre-sales engineer helping me build the entire demonstration. I started by discussing which artifacts should be created, what data I wanted to use, the demo flow, the reports I wanted to show, and the differentiation points I wanted to highlight.

With each interaction, Codex and I refined the solution. First, I invested time aligning on the solution design and proposed architecture. Then I asked Codex to actually build the demo. At the same time, I used Gemini to create a few presentation slides that would help me explain the demo flow to the customer.

A few hours later, Codex had finished building the demonstration. I would say it was around 80% ready.

I still made some final adjustments. Those human touches still matter. They are often what make the difference between a technically correct demo and a compelling customer conversation. I also made a few small changes to the slides.

But in just a few hours, I had a fully customized demo and a personalized presentation for that specific customer — something that previously would have taken days to build.

This is the future of our profession. But getting there requires more than simply giving people access to tools like Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude, or Codex.

Organizations need the right foundations in place. In this case, the call recording and transcript gave me the customer context. The CRM data gave me visibility into the customer's history and current situation. The MCP connection gave the AI agent access to the platform so it could actually build the demo.

All these pieces need to come together: customer context, internal data, secure integrations, AI tools, and people who know how to guide the process.

The future of pre-sales is not about spending 40% of the team's time alone building demos and presentations. It is about dramatically reducing that preparation time so solution engineers can spend more time with customers: understanding their business, their pain points, their priorities, and what they are really trying to solve.

Let’s look at a simple business case. Imagine a solution engineering team with 10 people that currently spends 30% of its time on solution preparation. If AI-enabled workflows reduce that preparation time to 15%, each SE gets back roughly 24 hours per month. Across the team, that is 240 hours per month — the equivalent of adding about 1.5 SEs of capacity without increasing headcount.

The teams that learn how to work this way will deliver more personalized, higher-quality customer experiences with far less manual effort. The teams that do not will spend more time and energy delivering less value.
